Executive Summary

The question behind the research

The city has always been a site of reinvention. Immigrants arrive, build, adapt — and in doing so, become load-bearing pillars of the urban economy. New York City's immigrant-owned micro and small enterprises are no exception: they are the connective tissue of neighbourhood economies, absorbing economic shocks in ways that large firms simply cannot. But today they face a disruption that is quieter, more diffuse, and harder to organise against — not a recession, not a pandemic, but the quiet, compounding force of artificial intelligence reshaping how business is done at every level.

This project investigates how AI adoption can be designed to build economic resilience among New York City's immigrant-owned small businesses — and what it takes to make that adoption last. The curriculum was built, piloted, and refined through the NYS Entrepreneurship Assistance Centers Program with Welcome to Chinatown.

The answer is not simply "more training." It is training designed with and for the learner — grounded in the lived reality of time-constrained, multilingual adult entrepreneurs who have already survived more than most (and informed by Kolb's experiential learning cycle and Fink's taxonomy of significant learning). The curriculum this research produces is, in that sense, both a scholarly artifact and a practitioner's wager: that the gap between knowing AI exists and actually using it can be closed, one workflow at a time.

Central Research Question
How can AI adoption for workflow optimization be designed and implemented to enhance the economic resilience of immigrant-owned micro and small businesses, and under what conditions can these gains scale into broader urban resilience?

    Economic Researcher & Curriculum Designer · Brooklyn, NY

    Some researchers study a problem from a distance. Daniel arrived inside one. As a Research & Curriculum Development Intern at Welcome to Chinatown, he has spent the past year working directly with 672 immigrant-owned small business owners across New York State — designing and delivering AI training that meets people where they are, not where the technology assumes they should be.

    His capstone research, Cyborg Enterprises and the Urban Future, investigates how AI adoption shapes economic resilience among New York City's most vulnerable small businesses, and builds the curriculum infrastructure to close the gap between AI's potential and its actual reach. The question, at its core, is not technical. It is institutional, pedagogical, and deeply human.

    Before graduate school, Daniel co-founded Munchies Grill & Bar in Accra, scaling to 1,000 meals a day with 25 staff. He has led play-based education programmes in rural Ghana, worked as a Research Assistant to Prof. Gayatri Spivak at Columbia University, and coordinated writing pedagogy for 1,400+ students annually at Ashesi University. The common thread: good design, grounded in real context, changes what is possible.

    Field Notes & Reflections from the Researcher
    On Positionality

    The position of the scholar-practitioner is not one I am a stranger to. I have long straddled academia and industry, but this project marked the first time I wore both hats simultaneously. It was an experience defined by tension — each perspective pressing against the other, each insisting on its own logic. As the academic embedded within a community support organisation running at pace, I encountered a world where speed is law and learning happens through motion — fast, imperfect, iterative. This stood in friction with the part of me that seeks theory, that leans on frameworks, that moves with deliberation and methodological care.

    But the tension proved generative. The academic lent the practitioner rigour and gave shape, language, and structure to instinct. The practitioner, in turn, taught the academic speed, intuition sharpened by necessity, and clarity forged in action.

    On My Evolving Relationship with AI

    My interest in artificial intelligence is recent, though my commitment to technological equity is not. I have never been an early adopter; if anything, I tend toward the cautious edge of diffusion. I remember a conversation in 2021 with a friend who was convinced that AI would transform everything. At the time, I met his conviction with scepticism, even mild amusement — another overhyped plaything of tech elites, I thought. Today, that position feels almost untenable.

    As a small business owner in Accra, I approached this project not only as a researcher, but as a participant. In many ways, I was my own first student. Coming from academia, my initial relationship with AI was marked by resistance — I worried about the erosion of skill, and at times cloaked my intimidation in ethical critique. These concerns were not unfounded, but they were incomplete. They reduced my engagement to a false binary: to accept uncritically or to reject entirely. This project has pushed me toward a third way: to engage critically.

    To engage AI through the lens of an educator is to recognise that adoption cannot be neutral. It must be accompanied by a commitment to governance, to regulation, and to shaping these tools toward socially beneficial and equitable ends. AI education, in this sense, must be inseparable from civic education. Otherwise, we risk teaching people to use systems they do not understand, cannot influence, and are ultimately subject to. And that has become my newfound conviction.
