Executive Summary

The question behind the research

The city has always been a site of reinvention. Immigrants arrive, build, adapt — and in doing so, become load-bearing pillars of the urban economy. New York City's immigrant-owned micro and small enterprises are no exception: they are the connective tissue of neighbourhood economies, absorbing economic shocks in ways that large firms simply cannot. But today they face a disruption unlike any that came before — not a recession, not a pandemic, but the quiet, compounding force of artificial intelligence reshaping how business is done at every level.

This project investigates how AI adoption for workflow optimization can be designed to enhance the economic resilience of immigrant-owned MSMEs in New York City, and develops a tailored AI training curriculum — designed, piloted, and refined through the NYS Entrepreneurship Assistance Centers Program with Welcome to Chinatown. It asks not just whether these businesses can adopt AI, but what institutional conditions, pedagogical frameworks, and support structures are required to make that adoption durable, equitable, and ultimately scalable into broader urban resilience.

The answer is not simply "more training." It is training designed with and for the learner — grounded in Kolb's experiential learning cycle, Fink's taxonomy of significant learning, and the lived reality of time-constrained, multilingual adult entrepreneurs who have already survived more disruptions than most. The curriculum this research produces is, in that sense, both a scholarly artifact and a practitioner's wager: that the gap between knowing AI exists and actually using it can be closed, one workflow at a time.

    Survey Data & Findings

    What 537 small businesses actually said

    Between January and April 2025, structured needs assessments reached 537 small business owners across 25 NYS Entrepreneurship Assistance Centers — surveyed in English, Spanish, and Chinese. The findings map both the challenges these businesses are navigating and the specific barriers standing between them and meaningful AI adoption. Every curriculum design decision follows from this data, not from assumption.

    Welcome to Chinatown Impact Study — further context on community need and programme reach
    💬
    The open-text responses revealed something the checkboxes couldn't hold. The core issue isn't rejection of AI — it's a missing on-ramp. Business owners described feeling overwhelmed by the pace and volume of new tools, not afraid of the technology itself. For Spanish- and Chinese-speaking owners, the language gap is specifically about training access — not tool interfaces. They lack self-directed learning resources in their own languages: videos, guides, tutorials they can work through independently. For artisan and maker businesses, the barrier cuts deeper still: a sense that AI belongs to a corporate world that doesn't speak to their craft. These nuances don't appear in the multiple-choice data. They shaped the curriculum's design at every level.

    Economic Researcher & Curriculum Designer · Brooklyn, NY

    Some researchers study a problem from a distance. Daniel arrived inside one. As a Research & Curriculum Development Intern at Welcome to Chinatown, he has spent the past year working directly with 537 immigrant-owned small business owners across New York State — designing and delivering AI training that meets people where they are, not where the technology assumes they should be.

    His capstone research, Cyborg Enterprises and the Urban Future, investigates how AI adoption shapes economic resilience among New York City's most vulnerable small businesses, and builds the curriculum infrastructure to close the gap between AI's potential and its actual reach. The question, at its core, is not technical. It is institutional, pedagogical, and deeply human.

    Before graduate school, Daniel co-founded Munchies Grill & Bar in Accra — scaling from start-up to 1,000 meals a day with 25 staff — and led a play-based education curriculum reaching 400 students in rural Ghana's cocoa communities. He has worked as a Research Assistant to Prof. Gayatri Spivak at Columbia University, and coordinated writing pedagogy for 1,400+ students annually at Ashesi University. The throughline in all of it: a conviction that good design, grounded in real context, changes what is possible.
